MONSTERS

In our pursuit of crafting engaging and immersive game experiences, we draw inspiration from the depth and richness of Norse and Scandinavian mythologies and folklore. These ancient narratives serve as pivotal sources that shape our creative direction, guiding the development of our forthcoming projects.

We present these entities as illustrative examples that encapsulate the broader conceptual vision of integrating mythical elements into our game worlds.

DRAUGAR

The Draugar, originating from Old Norse lore, epitomize revenants
and the spectral undead, haunting burial mounds in pursuit of guarded treasures.
These corporeal reanimations, distinct from ethereal ghosts, retain physical prowess
from their past lives. Signaled by corpses found in upright positions, their return
defies the natural course of death, embracing selfishness and malevolence. Ancient precautions,
like straw under shrouds and bound toes, aimed to confine these troublesome beings to their tombs,
yet their relentless presence straddles the line between life and death, striking fear with their
formidable and unsettling existence.

GJENGANGER

The Gjenganger, rooted in Old Norse, embodies a revenant's essence,
symbolizing "walking again" or returning after death.
Its corporeal form lacks spectral traits but exudes malevolence,
often tied to unresolved deaths or suicides. Stemming from Viking sagas,
this malevolent being, akin to the draugr, instilled fear, prompting extensive
measures to contain it within graves. Protection rituals involving crucifixes,
ymbols, and church traditions aimed to ward off its wrath, while superstitions
around marking death sites, called varps, persisted, believed to affect travelers' luck.
The Gjenganger remains a haunting specter of Norse folklore,
necessitating careful safeguards to evade its restless malevolence.

HULDRA

The Hulder, a seductive forest being of Scandinavian folklore,
draws her name from a root signifying "covered" or "secret".
Known as huldra in Norwegian folklore and under various names in Swedish and Sámi traditions,
she represents a collective race rather than an individual.
Often depicted as beautiful, the Hulder bears a striking inhuman feature—an animal's tail,
usually from a cow or fox, and a back resembling a hollowed-out tree.
Part of the rå, which includes aquatic beings like sjörå or havsfru and the bergsrå in caves,
the Hulder stands among these wardens or keepers.
Stories recount the Hulder's kindness toward charcoal burners,
watching over their kilns and receiving provisions in return,
showcasing her benevolent nature when treated respectfully.

MARE

Mares are malevolent spirits or goblins known for haunting individuals during sleep.
These entities, believed to ride upon sleeping victims, inflict distressing dreams known as "nightmares".
According to legend, Mares hover over sleepers,
inducing a sense of paralysis and panic, exerting a heavy weight from the feet to the chest.
This haunting presence often leaves victims feeling suffocated and drained of energy,
associated with terrifying dreams and a sense of breathlessness.

MYLINGS

Mylings, the spectral souls of unbaptized children in folklore,
are tragic beings born from infanticide, destined to roam until properly buried or named.
Created when unwanted children are killed or abandoned,
they haunt areas where they were buried—under floors, stone heaps, or in the woods.
Their eerie cries and night-time singing reveal their tragic origin and the mother's crime.
Legends depict Mylings tend to pursue lone wanderers,
leaping onto their backs and demanding to be carried to a graveyard for peace.
However, as they near their resting place, they grow increasingly heavy,
potentially causing carriers to sink into the soil.
ailure to reach the cemetery results in the Myling killing its carrier in a fit of rage.
These spirits are known for their mischievous pranks, transforming into animals or seeking revenge,
even draining their mother's blood in some tales.
Their haunting behavior, although tragic, portrays them as menacing and cruel entities in folklore.

Nökken

Nökken, male water spirits in folklore, played enchanting tunes on the violin,
luring people—especially women, children, and sometimes men—to waters where they risked drowning.
They appeared as elegant figures or shape-shifted,
often as a man playing the violin or a feared "brook horse".
Known for their dangerous music, they were most active on certain nights,
posing a threat to the unbaptized and pregnant women. Supposedly,
calling their name could defeat malicious Nökkens, leading to their demise.
These myths evolved post-Christianization, intertwining with tales of faeries and other entities.

NORNS

In Norse mythology, the Norns—powerful female beings—control destiny,surpassing even the gods in influence.
Three primary Norns, Urd, Verdandi, and Skuld, dwell near Yggdrasill,
nurturing the tree at the cosmos' center to prevent decay.
They visit newborns, shaping their futures, where some Norns bring tragedy while others are protective.
Represented as maidens of fate, they craft destiny through casting lots, weaving, or carving symbols,
symbolizing fate's construction. Though not objects of worship, the Norse viewed fate as implacable,
urging acceptance rather than seeking alteration. The Norns embody an unchangeable force shaping existence.
